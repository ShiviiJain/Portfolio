Uncovering the Pacific Crest Trail’s Litter Saga

The Pacific Crest Trail, a pristine 2,650-mile trek spanning from Mexico to Canada, is not just a hiker's paradise but a silent witness to human negligence. Tori McGruer and Macy Gustavus, two environmental scientists, embarked on a 6-month hike to uncover the facts about the litter along the Pacific Crest Trail (PCT) and came across some interesting findings. “Bandages, Balloons, Bullet Casings” presents an insightful journey of a meticulous mission to gather data, addressing the human impact on natural landscapes.

McGruer and Gustavus handpicked litter across the trail, conducting surveys every 10 miles. The article paints a vivid picture of the variety of the 1000+ pieces of litter they collected. A collection of common finds like snack wrappers, and cigarette butts to uncommon finds like mylar balloons, the PCT had it all. The study conducted surveys in 260 zones, of which 60% were found to be areas with high concentrations of litter. This data gives us an important connection between urban density and litter in areas which could be explored in depth. The duo categorized the waste into 15 distinct types using an app called Rubbish, ensuring the metric of the classification stayed the same. However, this method raises questions: How were the criteria for 15 different categories determined? Was the basis material, size, or impact of the environment? Furthermore, the sampling methodology warrants scrutiny. The account lacks clarity on whether certain areas were better evaluated than others. Were there natural landscape features that hindered a comprehensive exploration of litter along the trail? The knowledge of these aspects would have helped increase the accuracy of the findings.

Although this story is driven by data, it misses several opportunities to detail the broader implications of these facts. Analysis into the broader reason for the existence of these different kinds of litter at the PCT would have helped address the issue. The study also misses the exploration of any local conservation policies as well as the impact of such litter on local wildlife and the ecosystem. It would have also been helpful to have a comparison of the trash found in other trails with the PCT trash findings. Although the article is going for a peer review, it is vital to highlight that these findings will impact the long-term soil and water quality of the PCT. While “Bandages, Balloons, Bullet Casings” provides awareness for increased environmental responsibility, it doesn’t go into the future solutions of how to mitigate the problem. In uncovering the Pacific Crest Trail’s litter saga, we are compelled to tread thoughtfully, transforming our trails into testimonies of environmental respect.
